Diamond 1 · Discover — Diverging into the problem space
01 — The Problem

Nobody Designed For Us

Discovery begins where all honest design begins — with observation of the real human experience. Not in a lab. On the road. In traffic. Every day.

"We have been riding scooters since the 90s. Back then, the dashboard had only a speedometer. We almost never looked at it while riding."

"Now? Speed. Battery. GPS turn-by-turn. Incoming call. Left indicator on. Range left. All on a small screen. All fighting for your eyes. And here is the problem nobody talks about — to see that screen, you have to look DOWN."

"That one second your eyes are on the dashboard is the one second the road needed them."

What the research revealed

The everyday hazard nobody is measuring

⬇️
Head bows down
Every 3–5 minutes, the rider's head tilts 15–28° downward to read the dashboard. At speed, this is a blind moment.
⚠️
Not just at high speed
At 10 km/h in a gully — pothole. At 15 km/h in a colony — a child runs out. Speed is not the variable. Attention is.
👩
Women riders most affected
Observed: most women riders avoid navigation entirely. The system failed them so completely they stopped using it.
🔧
Not a software problem
Apps and interfaces have improved. The fundamental physical relationship between rider eyes and dashboard position has never changed since the 1970s.

Who the road hazard affects

The danger is not limited to the rider. The pedestrian, the child, the elderly person crossing — they are not the company's customer. So nobody designs for them.

🏍️
The rider
A fall, a fracture, two months off work. For most Indian riders the scooter is not a convenience — it is the only way to reach work. A small fracture is not a small problem.
🚶
The pedestrian
Clipped at a crossing. Knocked by a turning scooter. They are someone's bread earner too. They have a family waiting at home. Nobody designed for them.
👧
The child
Running onto the road in a housing colony at 15 km/h. The rider was looking down for one second. That one second was all it took.
👴
The elderly
Crossing without looking. Slower to react. Every blind moment from a distracted rider is a risk the most vulnerable road users absorb.
The industrial parallel — already solved

"I spent 20 years designing interfaces for industrial control rooms — environments where a wrong display decision causes an incident. The same class of problem exists in every two-wheeler dashboard in India. The industrial world learned this lesson in the 1990s through incident investigation. Two-wheeler design has never read those reports."

— Srinivas CV · HMI Designer · 20 years safety-critical systems

Diamond 1 · Define — Converging on the right problem
02 — Define

What Are We Really Solving?

The define phase resists jumping to solutions. It forces a precise framing of the problem — before a single concept is drawn. What is the root cause? Who is truly affected? What has been tried and why did it fail?

Key insights from discovery
I·01
This is not a navigation problem. It is a position problem.

Every navigation improvement — better maps, clearer turn arrows, voice guidance — leaves the fundamental issue untouched. The screen is in the wrong place. Moving it is the only fix.

I·02
The industry has never measured the physiological cost

No two-wheeler manufacturer conducts systematic research into rider head movement frequency, angle, or cumulative fatigue. The absence of data is not evidence the problem is small — it is evidence the question was never asked.

I·03
Software cannot solve a hardware misalignment

The dashboard sits below eye level because the front apron of every scooter was designed for mechanical components, not for human visual ergonomics. This is an industrial design problem — not a UX problem in the digital sense.

I·04
Women riders are the silent evidence of complete design failure

The observation that most women riders avoid navigation entirely is not a preference. It is a signal that the system failed them so completely they made a calculated trade-off: less information for more safety. That is the user compensating for bad design.

How Might We

HMW questions translate problems into open design opportunities without prescribing solutions prematurely.

HMW 01

How might we position dashboard information within the rider's natural forward gaze — requiring zero head movement?

HMW 02

How might we make the display readable in direct sunlight without increasing screen brightness?

HMW 03

How might we solve the physical eyeline problem with form design alone — zero new technology required?

HMW 04

How might we make the rider's speed state visible to the pedestrians and other road users sharing the road?

Problem Statement

Every two-wheeler dashboard in India is physically positioned below the rider's natural eyeline — forcing a head bow that creates a blind moment of 1–2 seconds, repeated every 3–5 minutes, on every ride, for 200 million riders. This is not a software failure. It is a 40-year-old industrial design oversight that has never been corrected because the affected users — riders, pedestrians, children — were never the priority of the design process.

Diamond 2 · Develop — Diverging into the solution space
03 — Develop

Exploring Solutions

The develop phase opens wide again — into the solution space. Multiple directions were explored, stress-tested against the problem statement, and evaluated. Not all ideas survived. Showing what was rejected is as important as showing what was kept.

Directions explored
✕ Rejected
Smartphone handlebar mount (higher position)
Raises the display but creates new problems — vibration, weather exposure, mounting fragility. Solves height, not integration. Remains an afterthought rather than designed ergonomics.
✕ Rejected
Helmet HUD (heads-up display in visor)
Technically elegant but costs ₹15,000–80,000+ per helmet. Excludes 99% of Indian riders immediately. Solves the right problem for the wrong market segment.
◑ Partial
Audio navigation only
Removes visual distraction but fails in Indian traffic noise conditions. No visual confirmation creates anxiety. Works as a complement — cannot be the primary solution.
◑ Partial
Haptic turn signals in grips
Innovative direction — vibration patterns to guide turns. Reduces glance frequency but does not address the need for speed, battery, and navigation reference information.
✓ Pursued
Cobra Hood — raised integrated display housing
Form-based solution. Raises the display to natural eyeline. Shields from sunlight. Requires zero new technology. Works on existing dashboard systems. Manufacturable at no additional component cost.
✓ Pursued
Public Speed Beacon — external speed indicator
Fundamentally new idea — makes speed state visible to the surrounding environment, not just the rider. Pedestrians, cyclists, and other road users can read a vehicle's speed state for the first time.
Precedent analysis — what already works

Strong design solutions borrow from proven models. These precedents validated the develop direction before any prototype was built.

Domain Precedent Transferable principle
Aviation Fighter pilot HUD — display projected at eyeline, pilot never looks down Information at natural gaze angle eliminates blind moments. Proven for 60 years.
Industrial HMI High Performance SCADA — display positioned within operator natural sightline Same cognitive principle. Same human factors research. Already applied in control rooms.
Automotive Car instrument clusters — positioned directly in forward-looking sight line of driver Car designers solved this correctly. Two-wheeler designers copied the car dashboard position without the car's eye geometry.
Architecture Cobra hood canopy — natural shade from direct overhead light The cobra's spread hood blocks light from above and creates a permanent shade envelope below. Applied to the instrument cluster housing.
Safety systems Ambulance markings, truck height indicators — making vehicle state visible to others Vehicle state as public information, not private cockpit data. Foundation of the speed beacon concept.

Diamond 2 · Deliver — Converging on the resolved solution
04 — Deliver

The Cogni Hood System

The deliver phase converges on the resolved, manufacturable, measurable solution. Four integrated innovations. Zero new technology. Pure design.

Cogni Hood CH-01 · Concept sketch · Srinivas CV · 2026

The four integrated innovations
1
Cobra Hood Canopy Core innovation

The cobra-shaped hood spreads above the instrument cluster creating a permanent shadow envelope. The display sits in the "throat" — shielded from direct sunlight at any angle. Noon sun, rain, oncoming high-beam — always readable. No brightness increase required. Pure form.

2
Hood Uplift — Near-HUD Position Ergonomic

The hood raises the display to the rider's natural forward gaze. Zero head tilt. Zero blind moment. The same principle aircraft instrument panels and car dashboards use — applied to two-wheelers for the first time. Showroom personalisation adjusts height to each rider at delivery.

3
Public Speed Beacon World first

A small ambient light on the front fairing broadcasts speed state to the surrounding environment — not just to the rider. Green: within limit. Amber: approaching. Red: over limit. Pedestrians and cyclists can read a scooter's speed state before stepping off the footpath. First vehicle in history to make speed a public signal.

4
Smart Camera Mirror Pods Safety

Camera pods replace physical mirrors — no vibration distortion at speed, wider field of view in low light, integrated cleanly into the cobra hood form language. Rear view feeds displayed directly in the rider's peripheral vision within the instrument console.

Design features
F·01
Zero new technology
The Cogni Hood is a form change — a shape redesign of the front fairing and instrument cluster housing. It requires no additional electronics, sensors, or software. Compatible with every existing dashboard system.
F·02
Showroom personalisation
Hood height adjusted to each rider at point of sale — the way premium car manufacturers calibrate seat and steering positions. A 5-foot-2 rider and a 6-foot rider receive a personalised ergonomic setup. No rider is stuck with a height that doesn't fit their eyeline.
F·03
Desert visibility
The deep hood canopy creates a light-controlled environment around the display — making the screen readable in conditions where current dashboards wash out completely. Designed for India's full climate range: noon sun, monsoon glare, dust, night.
F·04
This problem is 40 years old. The solution fits on one sketch.
No new materials. No new manufacturing. No new supply chain. Just a different shape — designed with the human being in mind rather than the mechanical components beneath it.

What this case study demonstrates
★
The industry skipped Discovery entirely

Two-wheeler dashboards were never the result of a design process centred on the rider. They were positioned where mechanical components allowed. The question "where do the rider's eyes naturally look?" was never asked in 40 years of product development.

★
The Define phase revealed a larger problem

The pedestrian, the child, the elderly person crossing — they are not the company's customer, so nobody designed for them. A properly framed problem statement includes everyone the product affects — not just the person who buys it.

★
The solution was always one form change away

This problem is 40 years old. The solution fits on one sketch. The cobra hood requires no new technology, no new electronics, no new cost. The only thing it required was someone asking the right question. That is what the Double Diamond is for.
