UX Philosophy & Ideology
The thinking that drives all the work
Core Belief
"The greatest UX failures are not the ones people complain about. They are the ones people have accepted as normal."
The Central Argument
Most design solves for what people want. This work solves for what people silently endure. The computing industry, the automotive industry, and the software industry have all normalised physiological harm — eye strain, blind riding moments, cognitive overload — because nobody measured what happened after launch.
The Three Questions Behind Every Project
Question 1
What does the user's body experience during sustained real-world use — not in a 30-minute lab test?
Question 2
What has the user normalised that they should never have had to accept?
Question 3
What would genuinely remove that normalised suffering — not as a toggle, but as the right default from day one?
Post-Market UX Surveillance
The discipline of measuring what products do to human beings after 6 months of daily use. Not in a lab. In real life. Over years. This is the missing discipline in consumer product design — and the thread that connects every project in this portfolio.
The Philosophy in One Line
"I don't serve to deserve something in return. I wish to work where everyone deserves the best."
Key Principles
Defaults are the product Normalisation masks harm Industrial precedent ignored Post-market surveillance missing Design for the human not the demo

Case Studies
Double Diamond methodology applied to the two core concepts
Methodology — Double Diamond
Every case study follows the Double Diamond framework — Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver. Not as decoration. As the structural backbone that reveals why the problem persisted unsolved, how the right question was framed, what solutions were rejected, and what measurable success looks like.
Case Study 1
Windows White Background — The Silent Needle
Discover: 50-year historical research, CVS statistics, 1.4B users affected. Define: 4 insights + HMW questions + problem statement. Develop: 4 ideation directions with rejected options, precedent analysis. Deliver: Warm off-white default #F5F0E8, contrast ratio reduction, systemic recommendations.
Case Study 2
Cogni Hood CH-01 — Integrated Desert Visibility Console
Discover: 20 years lived observation, blind moment quantified, 4 affected groups including pedestrians. Define: Root cause = position not navigation, HMW questions, problem statement. Develop: 6 directions including 4 rejected, precedent from aviation/SCADA/automotive. Deliver: 4 integrated innovations, validation metrics, measurable impact.
The Key Insight Both Cases Share
"In both cases, the industry skipped the Discovery phase entirely. The problem was never defined. So the solution was never found. That is what the Double Diamond reveals — and what this portfolio exists to fix."

Pause before every answer
Two seconds signals thinking not reacting. Confidence not speed.

End answers with a question

Never apologise for the concept
It is a strong proposal. Own it completely.
When you don't know — say so
Then say how you would find out. Honesty builds more credibility than bluffing.

Personal Story
The artist, the SCADA designer, the human factors advocate
The Arc
The Artist
Personal art and painting practice. A visual eye calibrated to light, contrast, and composition before any screen was involved.
2006 — The Canvas Changes
SCADA mimic design begins. Every control room screen becomes a painting with consequence. Art interest channelled entirely into HMI. The artist never left — the medium changed.
20 Years — The Lens Develops
Designing for operators whose lives depend on the interface. Learning to see what products silently do to human beings under stress. High Performance SCADA. Safety-critical stakes.
The WebApp Project — The Door Opens
First formal consumer UX project. The vocabulary of UX gives language to 20 years of applied human factors thinking. The transition becomes clear.
2026 — The Second Innings
UX certification in progress. Portfolio building. The second innings dedicated entirely to applying safety-critical design thinking to the products that affect millions of people daily.
The Three Screens on the Desk
Left screen: Gmail — the world to reach. Centre screen: live SCADA mimic in progress — 20 years of craft still running. Right screen: Lenovo monitor glowing pink-magenta — the exact Windows default luminance problem described in Case Study 1. The desk photo is the whole story in one frame.

What I do is different — find what is silently hurting millions of people, name it, solve it, give it back to them as relief they didn't know they needed. That is not product thinking. That is human-centered advocacy at scale."
